Looking for Specific Clues

While the Full Blood Count gives us a brilliant overview, sometimes we need to zoom in and look for very specific things. Our specialist tests help us diagnose inherited conditions, detect infections, and monitor inflammation in the body.

Red Cell Investigations: Haemoglobinopathy Screening

What are Haemoglobinopathies?

This is a group of inherited conditions that affect haemoglobin, the protein inside red blood cells that carries oxygen. They can cause the haemoglobin to be a different shape (a 'variant') or for not enough of it to be made ('thalassaemia').

Why do we screen?

People who are 'carriers' are usually completely healthy, but if two carriers have a baby, there is a 1 in 4 chance the baby could have a serious condition like Sickle Cell Disease or Thalassaemia Major. Our screening programme aims to identify couples at risk so they can make informed choices.

Who do we screen?

  • Antenatal Screening: All pregnant women are offered this screen early in pregnancy.
  • Pre-operatively: We may screen patients before an operation.
  • Newborn Screening: All babies are screened at around 5 days old as part of the national programme.

Hunting for Infection & Inflammation

Malaria Investigation

For patients with a fever and a history of travel to a country where malaria is common, this is an urgent test. We examine a blood film under the microscope to look for the tiny malaria parasites inside the red blood cells. A rapid diagnosis is critical for this serious illness.

Glandular Fever (IM)

Also known as Infectious Mononucleosis, this is a common viral illness. We can do a quick screening test on a blood sample to help confirm the diagnosis. A positive result is usually confirmed by looking at a blood film for the 'atypical' white cells that are characteristic of the infection.

ESR & Plasma Viscosity

The Erythrocyte Sedimentation Rate (ESR) and Plasma Viscosity (PV) are two tests that act as general 'inflammation markers'. They measure how quickly red cells fall in a tube or how thick the blood plasma is. A high result can be a sign of inflammation somewhere in the body, helping doctors diagnose and monitor conditions like temporal arteritis and rheumatoid arthritis.
