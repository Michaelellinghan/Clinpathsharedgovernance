Routine Coagulation Tests

The first step in assessing bleeding and clotting.

Our Core Clotting Tests

These tests are our first-line investigations into the clotting system. They provide a quick and effective overview, helping doctors to monitor medication, prepare patients for surgery, and decide if more specialist testing is needed.

Prothrombin Time (PT) and INR

The PT measures how long it takes for a clot to form in a blood sample. It specifically assesses the 'extrinsic' and common pathways of the clotting cascade. Its most frequent use is to calculate the International Normalised Ratio (INR), which is essential for monitoring patients on Warfarin therapy to ensure their blood is appropriately thinned.

Activated Partial Thromboplastin Time (APTT)

The APTT measures the 'intrinsic' and common pathways of the cascade. It is commonly used as a pre-operative screen to check for underlying bleeding tendencies and for monitoring patients on Heparin therapy. An unexpectedly prolonged APTT would trigger further investigation into specific clotting factor deficiencies, like Haemophilia.

Fibrinogen

Fibrinogen is a key protein (Factor I) that is converted into fibrin threads to form the structure of a blood clot. We measure the level of fibrinogen in the blood, which is particularly important in cases of major bleeding or a condition called Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation (DIC), where the clotting system becomes overactive and uses up all the fibrinogen.

D-Dimer

When a blood clot is broken down by the body, tiny protein fragments called D-Dimers are released. A normal D-Dimer level is very useful for helping to rule out the presence of a dangerous clot, such as a Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT) in the leg or a Pulmonary Embolus (PE) in the lungs. However, a high level can be caused by many other conditions, so it is less useful for confirming a clot.
