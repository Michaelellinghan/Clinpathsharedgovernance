Routine Coagulation Tests

Exploring the workhorse tests of the coagulation laboratory, what they measure, how they work, and how we interpret them to guide patient care.

Inside the Coagulation Lab: The Process

Pre-analytical: The Critical First Step

Accurate coagulation testing starts with a perfect sample. All tests are performed on a 2.7mL light blue top tube containing 3.2% sodium citrate anticoagulant. It is absolutely vital that the tube is filled correctly to the frosted line. This ensures the precise 9:1 ratio of blood to anticoagulant. An underfilled tube will have too much anticoagulant, which will bind excess calcium and falsely prolong all clotting times, leading to incorrect results and potentially inappropriate clinical action.

Analytical Method: Optical Detection

At NUH, we use Sysmex CS-series analysers. The primary method for PT, APTT, and Fibrinogen is optical clot detection. The analyser mixes patient plasma with activating reagents in a cuvette and shines a light beam through it. As a fibrin clot forms, the plasma becomes more turbid, scattering the light. The analyser detects this change, and the time taken to reach a pre-defined level of turbidity is recorded as the clotting time. For D-Dimer, a similar but more complex immuno-turbidimetric method is used.

The Routine Test Menu: A Detailed Guide

Prothrombin Time (PT/INR)13.5 s / 1.010-12.5 s

Prothrombin Time (PT) & INR

Principle: Measures the extrinsic and common pathways by adding Tissue Factor (Innovin reagent) to patient plasma.
Prolonged In: Warfarin therapy, liver disease, vitamin K deficiency, DIC, factor VII, X, V, II, I deficiency.

APTT32 s21-29 s

Activated Partial Thromboplastin Time

Principle: Measures the intrinsic and common pathways by adding a contact activator and phospholipid (Actin FS reagent) to patient plasma.
Prolonged In: Heparin therapy, lupus anticoagulant, haemophilia (FVIII/FIX deficiency), von Willebrand disease, DIC.

APTT 50:50 Mix28 s21-29 s

APTT 50:50 Mix (Correction Study)

Principle: Performed when there is an unexplained prolonged APTT. Patient plasma is mixed 1:1 with normal plasma (which contains 100% of all factors) and the APTT is repeated.
Interpretation: If the APTT 'corrects' into the normal range, it indicates the patient has a factor deficiency. If it fails to correct, it suggests the presence of an inhibitor (e.g., Lupus Anticoagulant).

Fibrinogen (Clauss)3.5 g/L1.8-4.0 g/L

Fibrinogen

Principle: Measures functional fibrinogen by adding a high concentration of thrombin to diluted plasma. The clotting time is inversely proportional to the fibrinogen concentration.
Low In: DIC, severe liver disease, massive transfusion.
High In: Inflammation (it's an acute phase reactant), pregnancy.

Thrombin Time (TT)16 s14-19 s

Thrombin Time

Principle: Measures the final step of coagulation - the conversion of fibrinogen to fibrin - by adding thrombin reagent to plasma.
Prolonged In: Heparin therapy (very sensitive), low or abnormal fibrinogen (dysfibrinogenaemia), high levels of FDPs.

TTPS17 s14-19 s

Thrombin Time Protamine Sulphate (TTPS)

Principle: A Thrombin Time test with added protamine sulphate, which neutralises any unfractionated heparin in the sample.
Interpretation: If the TT is prolonged but the TTPS is normal, it confirms heparin contamination as the cause of the prolonged result.

D-Dimer200 ng/mL<500 ng/mL

D-Dimer

Principle: An immunoturbidimetric assay that detects a specific degradation product of a cross-linked fibrin clot.
High In: VTE (DVT/PE), DIC, surgery, trauma, infection, cancer, pregnancy. Its main use is its high negative predictive value to rule out VTE in low-risk patients.

Clinical Interpretation in Practice

Warfarin Monitoring

A patient on Warfarin therapy for Atrial Fibrillation needs their dose adjusting. We analyse their PT and the analyser calculates the INR. If their target is 2.0-3.0, an INR of 1.5 is too low (risk of clotting) and an INR of 4.5 is too high (risk of bleeding). The result directly guides their medication dose.

Unexplained Bleeding

A patient has a prolonged APTT but a normal PT. This points to a problem in the intrinsic pathway. As per our algorithm, we would perform a 50:50 mixing study. If the APTT corrects, it suggests a factor deficiency (like Haemophilia). If it fails to correct, it suggests an inhibitor is present (like a Lupus Anticoagulant).

Suspected DIC

A patient with severe sepsis is bleeding from their line sites. We run a full screen. The classic pattern for Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation (DIC) is a prolonged PT and APTT, a low Fibrinogen, and a very high D-Dimer, reflecting a massive, uncontrolled activation of the entire clotting system.

Excluding a DVT

A patient presents to ED with a swollen calf. Their clinical risk score is low. We run a D-Dimer. A result of 250 ng/mL is below the 500 ng/mL cut-off. This has a very high negative predictive value, meaning it is extremely unlikely the patient has a DVT, and they can be safely discharged without needing a leg scan.

The TTP Conundrum

A patient presents with confusion and purpura. The FBC shows a critically low platelet count and the blood film shows red cell fragments. This is the classic picture of Thrombotic Thrombocytopenic Purpura (TTP), a medical emergency. However, the routine coagulation screen (PT, APTT, Fibrinogen) is usually completely normal. This is a key diagnostic feature that distinguishes TTP from DIC, where the clotting screen is deranged. The diagnosis relies on the FBC and film findings, not the clotting screen.
