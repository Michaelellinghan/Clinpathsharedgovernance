What is Haematology? The Science of Blood

Haematology is the comprehensive study of blood, the tissues that create it (like bone marrow), and the wide range of diseases and disorders that affect it. Think of us as the body's 'blood detectives'. From the moment a purple-top tube arrives in our lab, we begin a detailed investigation to analyse its contents. Our work is a crucial part of healthcare, providing vital information for diagnosing conditions ranging from simple anaemia to complex disorders like leukaemia and haemophilia.

The field is vast, covering everything from the automated counting of billions of cells to the intricate manual art of identifying abnormal cells under a microscope, and the complex pathways that govern how our blood clots. This page will give you an overview of the key areas within this fascinating discipline.

A Deeper Dive into Haematology

Routine Haematology: The Full Blood Count (FBC)

The cornerstone of any haematology lab is the Full Blood Count (FBC). This is one of the most frequently requested tests in medicine, providing a rapid and comprehensive snapshot of a patient's health. The long list of acronyms and numbers can seem daunting, but by following a structured approach, we can make sense of them all!

Our high-tech analysers, like the Sysmex XN-9100, use laser technology and electrical impedance to count and measure the three main cellular components of blood: red cells, white cells, and platelets.

Red Cell Parameters

These tests quantify the amount of red cells and haemoglobin. An abnormally low level is known as anaemia, while an abnormally high level is polycythaemia.

  • Haemoglobin (Hb): Measures the amount of oxygen-carrying protein. It's the primary indicator of anaemia.
  • Mean Corpuscular Volume (MCV): The average size of red cells. This is crucial for classifying anaemia: low MCV (microcytic) can suggest iron deficiency, while high MCV (macrocytic) points towards vitamin B12 or folate deficiency.
  • Haematocrit (Hct): The percentage of blood volume occupied by red cells.
  • Red Cell Count (RCC): The total number of red cells.

White Cell Parameters

A high white cell count (leukocytosis) often indicates infection or inflammation, while a low count (leukopenia) can be caused by various conditions, including viral infections or certain medications.

  • White Blood Cell Count (WCC): The total number of all white cells.
  • Differential: This is the breakdown of the WCC into its different types:
    • Neutrophils: The body's first line of defence against bacterial infection.
    • Lymphocytes: Key fighters of viral infections.
    • Monocytes, Eosinophils, Basophils: Specialised cells involved in cleaning up debris, fighting parasites, and allergic reactions.
  • Blasts: The presence of these immature cells in circulating blood is highly abnormal and requires urgent review for conditions like acute leukaemia.

Platelet Parameters

Platelets are tiny cell fragments vital for clotting. A low count is thrombocytopenia (risk of bleeding), and a high count is thrombocytosis.

  • Platelet Count: The total number of platelets. A low count can cause spontaneous bruising and bleeding.
  • Mean Platelet Volume (MPV): The average size of platelets. A high MPV can indicate the bone marrow is rapidly producing new, larger platelets.
