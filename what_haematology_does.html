The Journey of an FBC Sample

  1. Step 1: Arrival & Automated Sorting

    The journey begins when a blood sample arrives. It's booked into our system and placed on an automated track which sorts it for analysis.

  2. Step 2: The Full Blood Count (FBC)

    The sample's first stop is a high-tech analyser for a Full Blood Count. This machine counts and measures all the different blood cells, giving a vital snapshot of the patient's health.

  3. Step 3: Scientific Validation

    The results from the analyser are reviewed by a skilled Biomedical Scientist, checking for any flags or abnormalities that may require a closer look.

  4. Step 4: The Blood Film Review

    If the FBC is abnormal, a thin film of blood is made and stained. A scientist then examines the cells under a microscope, looking for visual clues that the machines can't see. This is the only test that is a direct follow-up from the FBC.

Other tests like Coagulation screens or Malaria checks are separate pathways, requested specifically by clinicians based on the patient's condition.

The Full Blood Count in Detail

The full blood count gives insight into the cellular components of blood including white blood cells, red blood cells, and platelets, providing numerical values relating to these cells (e.g. amount of them, size, contents). These results provide vital clues to the presence of underlying pathology. The long list of acronyms and numbers can seem daunting at first, however, by following a structured approach you can make sense of them all!

Red Cell Parameters

Red cell tests quantify the amount of haemoglobin and red cells present. An abnormally low number is known as anaemia, while an abnormally high number is known as polycythaemia. These tests also provide crucial insights into the underlying causes by looking at the average size of the cells and how much haemoglobin is contained within them.

  • Haemoglobin (Hb): The amount of oxygen-carrying protein in whole blood. This is the key value for diagnosing anaemia or polycythaemia.
  • Mean Corpuscular Volume (MCV): The average size of the red cells. A low MCV (microcytic) can suggest iron deficiency, while a high MCV (macrocytic) can suggest B12 or folate deficiency.
  • Haematocrit (Hct): The percentage of the blood sample that is made up of red cells. A high Hct can increase blood viscosity.
  • Red Cell Count (RCC): The total number of red cells present per unit volume of blood.
  • Red Cell Distribution Width (RDW): A measure of the variation in red cell size. A high RDW means there is a large variation in cell sizes (anisocytosis), which can be seen in conditions like iron deficiency or mixed-cause anaemias.
  • Reticulocyte Count: The number of immature red cells. A high count in an anaemic patient suggests the bone marrow is working hard to produce new cells, pointing to blood loss or destruction (haemolysis) in the periphery.

White Cell Parameters

A high total white cell count is known as leukocytosis, while a low count is leukopenia. The differential, which breaks down the count into different cell types, is crucial for diagnosis.

  • White Blood Cell Count (WCC): The total number of all white blood cells. Often raised in infection and inflammation.
  • Neutrophils: These make up the majority of white cells and are the first responders to bacterial infections. A high count is called neutrophilia.
  • Lymphocytes: Key players in the immune system, fighting viral infections. A high count (lymphocytosis) is common in viral illnesses like glandular fever.
  • Monocytes, Eosinophils & Basophils: These are less common white cells. Monocytes are the "clean-up crew," eosinophils are involved in allergic reactions and fighting parasites, and basophils play a role in allergic responses.
  • Blasts: These are immature cells normally found only in the bone marrow. Their presence in circulating blood is a serious finding that requires urgent investigation for conditions like acute leukaemia.

Platelet Parameters

Platelets are small cell fragments that are essential for blood clotting. A low platelet count is thrombocytopenia (increased bleeding risk), while a high count is thrombocytosis.

  • Platelet Count: The number of platelets in the blood. A low count can cause bruising and spontaneous bleeding, while a very high count can increase the risk of unwanted clotting.
  • Mean Platelet Volume (MPV): The average size of platelets. A high MPV can indicate that the bone marrow is producing larger, younger platelets in response to platelet destruction in the circulation.
