Understanding Haematology

We're the blood detectives, solving medical mysteries one cell at a time.

What is Haematology?

Haematology is the branch of medicine all about your blood and the bits of your body that make it, like your bone marrow. When your doctor sends a blood sample to the lab, it's our team of 'blood detectives' who analyse it to find clues about your health.

Our most common investigation is the Full Blood Count (FBC). Think of it as a census of your blood, counting all the different types of cells. The numbers we get are vital for diagnosing a huge range of conditions, from simple anaemia to serious infections and even leukaemia.

The Full Blood Count: A Closer Look

Red Blood Cells (RBCs)

These are your body's oxygen delivery drivers. We count them, check their size, and measure their haemoglobin (the protein that carries oxygen). Too few can mean you have anaemia, making you feel tired and breathless.

White Blood Cells (WBCs)

Your body's defence force against infections. A high count can signal your body is fighting something off. Very high or very low counts, or the presence of strange-looking cells, can point towards more serious conditions like leukaemia.

Platelets

These tiny cells are your personal repair crew, plugging leaks when you get a cut. If you have too few, you might bruise easily. Too many can increase the risk of unwanted blood clots.

Beyond the Count: The Art of Morphology

Sometimes, the numbers from our analysers don't tell the whole story. If a result is unusual, our highly skilled biomedical scientists will make a 'blood film'. This involves spreading a tiny drop of blood onto a glass slide, staining it with special dyes, and looking at it under a microscope.

This is called morphology, and it's where the real detective work happens. We're looking at the shape, size, and appearance of the cells. Are the red cells pale or oddly shaped? Are there strange-looking white cells? These visual clues are crucial for diagnosing conditions like sickle cell anaemia, leukaemia, and malaria.
