Antenatal Screening

Protecting the health of mother and baby through specialised blood testing.

The Screening Pathway

The aim of the routine antenatal blood screening service is to identify pregnancies that may be at risk of Haemolytic Disease of the Fetus & Newborn (HDFN). This is a condition where antibodies from the mother cross the placenta and attack the baby's red blood cells, potentially causing severe anaemia. The screening pathway involves tests at two key stages:

Booking Appointment (8-12 Weeks)

At the first booking appointment, a sample is taken for a full blood group and antibody screen. This initial test establishes the mother's ABO and RhD group and checks for any pre-existing clinically significant antibodies.

28 Weeks Gestation

The blood group and antibody screen is repeated for all pregnant women. This is to check if any new antibodies have developed during the pregnancy. For D-negative women, this sample is taken before they receive their routine dose of Anti-D prophylaxis.

Key Tests and Interventions Explained

Fetal RHD Screening (FDS)

For D-negative mothers, we can now perform a test on their blood to predict the baby's D type. This highly specialised test analyses fragments of fetal DNA present in the maternal circulation from around 11 weeks gestation. If the fetus is predicted to be D-negative, the mother does not require Anti-D injections, avoiding unnecessary treatment.

Anti-D Prophylaxis

Anti-D immunoglobulin is a sterile solution of human antibodies that prevents a D-negative mother from forming her own powerful Anti-D antibodies if she is exposed to D-positive fetal cells. It is given in two situations:

  • RAADP (Routine Antenatal Anti-D Prophylaxis): A dose is given to eligible D-negative women at 28 weeks gestation.
  • PSE (Potential Sensitising Events): A dose is also required after any event during pregnancy that could cause fetal blood to enter the maternal circulation, such as a trauma or significant bleed.

Fetomaternal Haemorrhage (FMH) Test

After a potential sensitising event from 20 weeks gestation, a maternal blood sample is tested to estimate the volume of fetal blood that may have entered her circulation. This is most commonly done using the Kleihauer test, a specialised staining technique that makes fetal cells visible under a microscope. The size of the bleed is calculated so that the correct dose of Anti-D can be administered to ensure full protection.
