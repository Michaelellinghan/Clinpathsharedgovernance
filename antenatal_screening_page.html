Antenatal Screening

Protecting the health of mother and baby through early testing.

Why do we screen in pregnancy?

Antenatal screening is a series of tests offered during pregnancy to identify any potential health problems that could affect you or your baby. By identifying these risks early, we can offer information, support, and treatment to give your baby the best possible start in life. All screening is optional, and your midwife will discuss all the tests with you.

Key Screening Tests

Blood Group & Antibody Screen

At your booking appointment, and again at 28 weeks, we test your blood to find out your ABO and RhD blood group. This is vital in case you need a blood transfusion. We also check for any unusual blood group antibodies that could potentially cross the placenta and affect the baby's red blood cells, a condition called Haemolytic Disease of the Fetus and Newborn (HDFN).

For RhD Negative Mums: Anti-D

If you are RhD negative, you'll be offered injections of a substance called Anti-D immunoglobulin during pregnancy and after birth if your baby is RhD positive. This prevents your immune system from producing antibodies that could be harmful in future pregnancies. We can also now offer a test to predict your baby's D type from your own blood sample.

The Kleihauer Test (FMH Screen)

Following a potential sensitising event (such as a trauma or a bleed during pregnancy or delivery), there's a risk that some of the baby's blood cells can enter the mother's circulation. This is called a feto-maternal haemorrhage (FMH). The Kleihauer test is a special staining technique performed on a maternal blood film. Fetal red cells are resistant to acid elution and stain pink, whereas adult red cells become pale "ghost" cells. By counting the number of fetal cells seen, we can estimate the size of the FMH. This allows us to calculate the correct dose of Anti-D to give to neutralize the fetal cells and prevent the mother from becoming sensitized.
