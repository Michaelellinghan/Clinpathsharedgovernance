The Group & Screen

The cornerstone of safe transfusion practice.

What is a Group and Screen?

The "Group and Screen" (G&S) is one of the most common tests we perform. It's the first and most critical step in pre-transfusion testing. The test has two essential parts: determining the patient's blood group and screening their plasma for any unexpected red cell antibodies.

The Two Parts of the Test

1. The 'Group' (ABO and D Typing)

This part of the test determines the patient's blood group. We perform two checks:

  • Forward Group: We test the patient's red blood cells to see which antigens (A, B, and D) are present on their surface.
  • Reverse Group: We test the patient's plasma to see which antibodies (Anti-A and Anti-B) they have.

According to Landsteiner's Law, the forward and reverse group results must be a mirror image of each other for the group to be valid. For example, a group A patient will have A antigens on their cells and Anti-B antibodies in their plasma.

2. The 'Screen' (Antibody Screen)

This test looks for "atypical" or "unexpected" antibodies in the patient's plasma. These are antibodies against other red cell antigens outside of the ABO system.

Patients can develop these antibodies after being exposed to red cells that are different from their own, which can happen during:

  • A previous blood transfusion
  • Pregnancy

If the screen is positive, we must perform further tests to identify the specific antibody. This is crucial because if a patient receives blood with the corresponding antigen, it can cause a serious transfusion reaction. Finding and identifying antibodies before a transfusion is a key part of our job.
