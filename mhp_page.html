Massive Haemorrhage Protocol (MHP)

Our emergency response for critical bleeding.

What is a Massive Haemorrhage?

A massive haemorrhage is defined as the loss of 50% of a patient's blood volume within 3 hours, or a bleeding rate of 150 ml per minute. It is a life-threatening medical emergency that requires an immediate and coordinated response. The Transfusion laboratory plays a critical role in providing blood components rapidly to sustain life.

The Laboratory Response

Activation

The MHP is activated by the senior clinician in charge of the patient by calling the switchboard. A dedicated bleep is set off in the transfusion lab, which takes absolute priority over all other work. We immediately begin preparing emergency blood components.

Pack 1: The Initial Response

There is no time to perform a group and screen. We immediately issue a pre-prepared pack containing:

  • 4 units of Red Blood Cells: These are group O. For females under 50, we issue O Negative blood. For males and females over 50, we issue O Positive blood to conserve stocks of O Negative.
  • 4 units of Fresh Frozen Plasma (FFP): The plasma is pre-thawed and ready for immediate use to help with clotting.

Pack 2 and Beyond: Targeted Support

While Pack 1 is being transfused, we urgently process the patient's blood sample. Once we know their blood group, we switch to providing group-specific blood. Subsequent packs are guided by laboratory results and clinical need, and may contain:

  • More Red Blood Cells
  • More FFP
  • Platelets
  • Cryoprecipitate (a concentrated source of clotting factors)

The haematology registrar is informed and provides expert guidance throughout the event, liaising between the clinical team and the laboratory to ensure the right components are given at the right time.

Stand Down

The clinical team will "stand down" the MHP when the bleeding is under control. We then account for all issued units, manage any remaining stock, and ensure all documentation is complete for traceability.
