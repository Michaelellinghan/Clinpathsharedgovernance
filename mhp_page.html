Massive Haemorrhage Protocol (MHP)

Our emergency response for critical bleeding.

Recognising a Massive Haemorrhage

A massive haemorrhage is a life-threatening medical emergency defined as the loss of 50% of a patient's blood volume within 3 hours, or a continuous bleeding rate of â‰¥150 ml/min. Early recognition and activation of the MHP is critical for patient survival and requires a coordinated, multidisciplinary approach.

The Team Response & Communication

When the MHP bleep is activated, it triggers an immediate response. This is a high-priority event that overrides all other routine work. Clear and concise communication is essential. The laboratory staff will confirm the patient's details and location, and every component issued is meticulously documented on the Massive Haemorrhage and Emergency Blood Record Sheet to ensure full traceability.

MHP Pathways: Tailored to the Situation

The laboratory response is tailored to the specific clinical scenario. The contents of the MHP packs and the overall strategy can vary significantly depending on the cause and location of the bleed.

Trauma Haemorrhage (ED/Resus)

For trauma patients arriving in the Emergency Department, a standardised, aggressive approach is used with pre-defined packs to treat shock and coagulopathy early.

Pack 1

  • 4 units Red Cells (O Pos/Neg)
  • 4 units FFP

Pack 2

  • 6 units Red Cells (Group Specific)
  • 4 units FFP
  • 1 unit Platelets

Pack 3 & Beyond

  • As per Pack 2, plus:
  • 2 units Cryoprecipitate

Obstetric, Surgical & Cardiac Haemorrhage

For non-traumatic bleeds in areas like obstetrics or theatres, the protocol is more flexible. The initial response focuses on providing red cells quickly, while subsequent components are guided by laboratory results and point-of-care testing to provide targeted therapy.

  • Initial Step: Provide 4-6 units of emergency or group-specific Red Cells immediately.
  • Subsequent Support: FFP, Platelets, and Cryoprecipitate are issued based on the results of coagulation screens and in discussion with the Haematology Registrar.
  • Cardiac Surgery: Often uses point-of-care machines (ROTEM/TEG) to guide specific product usage, such as Cryoprecipitate for low fibrinogen.

Paediatric Haemorrhage

For children, pre-defined adult packs are not used. All component doses are carefully calculated based on the child's weight (e.g., 20ml/kg for red cells and FFP). This ensures life-saving treatment without the risk of fluid overload. The principles of using O Negative red cells first and switching to group-specific remain the same.
