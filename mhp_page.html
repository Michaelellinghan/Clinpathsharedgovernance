Massive Haemorrhage Protocol (MHP)

Our emergency response for critical bleeding.

What is a Massive Haemorrhage?

A massive haemorrhage is a life-threatening medical emergency defined as the loss of 50% of a patient's blood volume within 3 hours, or a continuous bleeding rate of â‰¥150 ml/min. When this occurs, the senior clinician activates the MHP, which triggers an immediate, coordinated response from the transfusion laboratory and the wider clinical team.

The Laboratory Response: MHP Packs

The laboratory response is structured into 'packs' of blood components. The contents of these packs depend on whether the bleed is due to trauma (e.g., an accident) or is non-traumatic (e.g., a post-operative bleed).

Pack 1

IMMEDIATE ISSUE

  • 4 units Red Cells (O Positive for males/females >50, O Negative for females <50)
  • 4 units FFP (Pre-thawed Group A)

Pack 2

Once Group is Known

  • 6 units Red Cells (Group Specific)
  • 4 units FFP
  • 1 unit Platelets

Pack 3 & Beyond

Guided by Results

  • 6 units Red Cells
  • 4 units FFP
  • 1 unit Platelets
  • 2 units Cryoprecipitate

Pack 1 (No Valid Sample)

IMMEDIATE ISSUE

  • 4 units Red Cells (Appropriate Group O)

Pack 1 (Valid Sample)

RAPID ISSUE

  • 6 units Red Cells (Group specific / crossmatched)

For non-traumatic bleeds, subsequent components (FFP, Platelets, Cryoprecipitate) are issued based on clinical need and laboratory results, guided by the Haematology Registrar.

Paediatric Emergencies

For children, the MHP is modified. Component doses are not given in standard packs but are carefully calculated based on the child's weight to prevent over-transfusion. The principles remain the same: provide O negative red cells and other components immediately, then switch to group-specific products as soon as possible.
