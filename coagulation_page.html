The Clotting Cascade

Investigating how your blood stops a leak.

What is Coagulation?

Coagulation is your body's amazing process for stopping bleeding. When you get a cut, a complex chain reaction, known as the 'clotting cascade', kicks off. It involves lots of different proteins in your blood called clotting factors, which work together with platelets to form a solid clot and seal the injury.

Our job in the lab is to test how well this cascade is working. It's crucial for patients who are about to have surgery, those with unexplained bruising or bleeding, and for monitoring people on blood-thinning medications like warfarin.

Our Main Clotting Tests

The Coagulation Screen (PT & APTT)

This is our first-line investigation. We perform a couple of tests that time different parts of the clotting cascade. The Prothrombin Time (PT) and the Activated Partial Thromboplastin Time (APTT) tell us if there are any delays in the process, which could point to a missing clotting factor or another issue.

The INR (International Normalised Ratio)

If you're on a blood-thinner called Warfarin, you'll be very familiar with the INR. It's a calculation based on the PT result that allows us to standardise the measurement across all labs. It tells your doctor if your Warfarin dose is just right â€“ not too high and not too low.

D-Dimer

When a blood clot breaks down, it releases tiny fragments called D-Dimers. We can measure these in the blood. A normal D-Dimer level is very useful for ruling out the presence of a dangerous clot, like a Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT) in the leg. However, a high level can be caused by many things, so it's just one piece of the puzzle.

Fibrinogen

Fibrinogen is one of the key building blocks of a blood clot. We can measure how much of it is in your blood. This is particularly important in cases of major bleeding or a condition called Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation (DIC), where the body's clotting system goes into overdrive.
