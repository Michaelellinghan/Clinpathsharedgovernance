The Coagulation Cascade

Unravelling the complex and elegant system of haemostasis, from routine screening to specialist investigations of bleeding and clotting disorders.

The Science of Haemostasis: A Detailed Look

Haemostasis is the body's rapid, localised, and carefully regulated process for stopping bleeding. It's a delicate and dynamic balance between pro-coagulant (clot-forming) and anti-coagulant/fibrinolytic (clot-busting) systems. Our job in the coagulation laboratory is to investigate when this intricate balance goes wrong, leading to either bleeding or thrombosis. The process can be broken down into key stages:

1. Primary Haemostasis: Forming the Platelet Plug

This is the immediate response to vascular injury, involving platelets and the blood vessel wall. Its goal is to quickly form an unstable platelet plug to block the breach.

The Vascular Phase: Vasoconstriction

Immediately upon injury to a blood vessel's endothelial lining, a neurogenic reflex causes the smooth muscle in the vessel wall to contract. This vasoconstriction narrows the lumen of the vessel, physically reducing blood flow to the damaged area, which minimises immediate blood loss and creates favourable conditions for the subsequent steps.

The Platelet Phase: Adhesion, Activation, Aggregation

This is where platelets become the heroes. In their resting state, they are smooth, disc-shaped cells. Upon injury:

  1. Adhesion: The damage exposes subendothelial collagen. Circulating von Willebrand Factor (vWF) acts like molecular glue, binding to this collagen and then tethering passing platelets via their GpIb receptor.
  2. Activation: This binding triggers the platelets to change shape, becoming spiky spheres with extended pseudopods to cover a larger surface area. They then release the contents of their granules (ADP, thromboxane Aâ‚‚, serotonin), which act as powerful chemical signals to recruit more platelets to the site.
  3. Aggregation: These chemical signals activate other passing platelets, causing them to express the GpIIb/IIIa receptor. Fibrinogen acts as a bridge, linking two GpIIb/IIIa receptors on adjacent platelets. This platelet-fibrinogen-platelet linkage builds up a mass of aggregated platelets, forming the primary haemostatic plug.

2. Secondary Haemostasis: The Fibrin Clot

The primary platelet plug is unstable and can be easily dislodged. Secondary haemostasis is the process of reinforcing this plug with a mesh of insoluble fibrin, creating a stable clot. This is achieved through the coagulation cascade.

The Coagulation Cascade: Intrinsic, Extrinsic & Common Pathways

This traditional model explains how a series of inactive enzymes (zymogens), known as clotting factors, are activated in a stepwise sequence. Our routine lab tests (APTT and PT) are based on this model.

  • Extrinsic Pathway: Triggered by trauma. Damaged cells release Tissue Factor (Factor III), which activates Factor VII. This is a very rapid, explosive start to clotting. We measure this with the Prothrombin Time (PT).
  • Intrinsic Pathway: Activated when blood comes into contact with a negatively charged surface (like the exposed collagen). It involves Factors XII, XI, IX, and VIII. We measure this with the Activated Partial Thromboplastin Time (APTT).
  • Common Pathway: Both pathways converge at the activation of Factor X. Activated Factor X (Xa), along with its co-factor Va, converts Prothrombin (Factor II) into its active form, Thrombin (IIa). Thrombin is the key enzyme in the final step.

The Final Step: Thrombin acts on soluble Fibrinogen (Factor I), cleaving it into insoluble fibrin monomers. These monomers spontaneously polymerise to form a weak fibrin mesh. Thrombin also activates Factor XIII, which cross-links the fibrin strands, creating a strong, stable, insoluble fibrin clot that traps red cells and platelets, effectively sealing the wound.

3. Fibrinolysis: Clot Dissolution

A clot cannot be a permanent fixture. Once the vessel has repaired itself, the process of fibrinolysis begins to break down the clot and restore normal blood flow. This process is just as crucial as clot formation.

Plasmin: The Clot Buster

The key enzyme in this process is Plasmin. It circulates as an inactive precursor, Plasminogen, which gets incorporated into the forming clot. Endothelial cells slowly release Tissue Plasminogen Activator (tPA), which binds to the fibrin in the clot and activates the trapped plasminogen, converting it to plasmin.

D-Dimers: The Footprint of a Clot

Plasmin acts like a molecular scissor, chopping up the cross-linked fibrin mesh into smaller pieces known as Fibrin Degradation Products (FDPs). One specific FDP, formed from the breakdown of a cross-linked clot, is the D-Dimer fragment. The presence of D-Dimers in the blood is proof that a fibrin clot has been formed and is being broken down somewhere in the body. This is why the D-Dimer test is so useful for investigating conditions like Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT) and Pulmonary Embolism (PE).
