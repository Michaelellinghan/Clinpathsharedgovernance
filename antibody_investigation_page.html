Antibody Investigation

The detective work of transfusion science.

When the Screen is Positive

When a patient's antibody screen is positive, our investigation begins. The goal is to identify the exact specificity of the antibody to ensure we can provide antigen-negative blood, preventing a potentially harmful transfusion reaction. This process requires a combination of specialist reagents, precise techniques, and expert interpretation.

The Investigation Toolkit

We use several techniques to build a complete picture of the antibody's behaviour. Full antibody investigations involve testing the patient's plasma against a panel of at least 8 different group O cells.

Indirect Antiglobulin Technique (IAT)

This is our primary method. It detects clinically significant IgG antibodies that react at body temperature (37Â°C). The test involves incubating patient plasma with panel cells in a specialized gel card incubator before centrifugation.

Enzyme Technique

Treating the panel cells with an enzyme (like papain) can enhance the reactions of some antibodies (especially those in the Rh system) while destroying the antigens for others (like Duffy and MNS systems). This helps us differentiate between multiple antibodies or confirm a suspected specificity.

Step-by-Step Detective Work

  1. 1. Run the Panels & Auto-Control

    We test the patient's plasma against our panel cells using both IAT and enzyme techniques. We also perform an "auto-control" by testing the patient's plasma against their own cells. A positive auto-control may indicate an auto-antibody is present.

  2. 2. Interpret the Pattern

    We carefully record the pattern of positive and negative reactions. By cross-referencing this pattern with the known antigen profiles of the panel cells, we can begin to identify the antibody. For example, if the plasma reacts with all three Fy(a+) cells on the panel but none of the Fy(a-) cells, we suspect the presence of Anti-Fya.

  3. 3. Confirm Specificity

    To be certain of an antibody's identity, strict criteria must be met. Specificity can only be assigned when the antibody reacts with at least two panel cells that have the target antigen, and does not react with at least two panel cells that lack the antigen. We must also exclude the presence of any other clinically significant antibodies that could be masked by the initial reaction pattern.

  4. 4. Patient Phenotyping

    The final confirmation step is to test the patient's own red blood cells. A patient cannot make an allo-antibody to an antigen they possess. Therefore, we must prove their cells are negative for the corresponding antigen to confirm the antibody's identity.
