Antibody Investigation

The detective work of transfusion science.

What happens when the antibody screen is positive?

When a patient's antibody screen is positive, it means we've detected an unexpected antibody. Our next job is to identify exactly what it is. This is like a form of detective work. We test the patient's plasma against a panel of reagent red cells. Each vial of cells in the panel is like a 'suspect' with a known set of antigens (identifying features). By seeing which cells the antibody reacts with, we can deduce its identity.

The Investigation Process

1. Running the Panel

We test the patient's plasma against a panel of 10-16 different group O reagent red cells. Each of these panel cells has a known phenotype, meaning we know which antigens (like C, c, E, e, K, k, Fya, Fyb, Jka, Jkb, etc.) are on its surface. We incubate the patient plasma with each of these cells and look for a reaction (agglutination).

2. Pattern Recognition

We record the pattern of positive and negative reactions. We then look at the antigen sheet that comes with the panel and try to find an antibody specificity that matches the reaction pattern. For example, if the plasma reacts with every cell that is K positive and none of the cells that are K negative, we can be confident the patient has Anti-K.

3. Confirmation and Exclusion

To confirm the antibody's identity, we must show that the patient's own red cells lack the corresponding antigen (you can't make an antibody to an antigen you have). We perform a phenotype on the patient's cells. We also have to exclude the presence of other, underlying antibodies that might be masked by the first one.

4. Ensuring Safety

Once an antibody is identified, we update the patient's record with a special requirement. For the rest of their life, they must receive blood that is negative for that specific antigen. This prevents a transfusion reaction and ensures their safety.
