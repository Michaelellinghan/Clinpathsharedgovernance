Haemostasis & Thrombophilia

A deeper look into bleeding and clotting disorders.

Getting the Balance Right

Haemostasis is the scientific term for stopping bleeding. Thrombophilia describes a condition where the blood has an increased tendency to form clots. These specialised tests investigate both sides of this delicate balance and are requested by a Consultant Haematologist when a routine screen is abnormal or there's a strong clinical suspicion of an underlying disorder.

Investigating a Bleeding Problem

Platelet Function Analysis (PFA)

This is a screening test to see how well platelets are working. We aspirate a blood sample through a tiny hole coated with substances that activate platelets. The machine times how long it takes for the platelets to form a plug and stop the flow. A prolonged time suggests the platelets aren't sticking together properly.

Von Willebrand Disease (VWD) Investigation

VWD is the most common inherited bleeding disorder, caused by a problem with Von Willebrand Factor (VWF), a 'glue-like' protein that helps platelets stick to an injury site. We perform tests to measure both the amount of VWF protein (Antigen test) and how well it's working (Activity test) to diagnose and classify the condition.

Specific Factor Assays & Inhibitor Screens

To diagnose conditions like Haemophilia, we mix a patient's plasma with plasma that is specifically missing one factor (e.g., Factor VIII deficient plasma). The amount of correction in the clotting time tells us how much of that factor the patient has. For an inhibitor screen, we mix patient plasma with normal plasma. If an inhibitor is present, it blocks the factors in the normal plasma too, and the clotting time won't correct as expected.

Investigating a Clotting Problem (Thrombophilia)

Thrombophilia Screen

For patients with unexplained clots, we look for inherited deficiencies in the body's natural anticoagulants. Antithrombin, Protein C, and Protein S act as the 'brakes' on the clotting system. We measure if there's enough of these proteins and if they're working correctly. We also screen for common genetic mutations (like Factor V Leiden) that make clotting more likely.

Lupus Anticoagulant

Despite its name, this antibody actually *increases* the risk of clotting. It interferes with phospholipid-dependent clotting tests in the lab. We confirm its presence using a two-step process: first, a sensitive screening test (like a DRVVT), and then a confirmation test with extra phospholipid, which corrects the clotting time if a Lupus Anticoagulant is present.

Monitoring Anticoagulants (Anti-Xa Assay)

For patients on certain blood thinners (like LMWH, Apixaban, or Rivaroxaban), we can measure the drug's effect using an Anti-Xa assay. This test measures how much Factor Xa (a key clotting enzyme) is being blocked by the medication. This helps doctors ensure the dose is just right - effective enough to prevent clots, but not so high as to cause bleeding.
