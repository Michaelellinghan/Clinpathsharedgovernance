A Guide to Understanding Scatterplots

Our advanced analysers use lasers and special dyes to look at your blood cells one by one. The results are shown as patterns called scatterplots, where each dot is a single cell. These patterns help scientists quickly see if your blood cells are healthy.

The White Blood Cell Family Portrait (WDF)

What We're Looking At

Think of this as a family photo of your different white blood cells, which are your body's infection fighters. The machine sorts them into groups based on how complex they are inside and what they're made of. In a healthy person, we see clear clusters for each "family member":

  • Lymphocytes (Blue): The smart cells of your immune system.
  • Monocytes (Green): The 'clean-up crew' that eats up germs.
  • Neutrophils (Pink): Your first-responders to infection.
  • Eosinophils (Red): Specialists that fight parasites and allergies.

The Head Count (WNR)

What We're Looking At

This is the most accurate way the machine gets a total 'head count' of all your white blood cells. It uses a strong reagent that strips away the outside of the cells, leaving only the nucleus (the cell's control centre). This simple method avoids errors. It's also excellent at spotting two specific cell types:

  • Main WBC Cluster (Light Blue): The nuclei of most of your white cells all grouped together.
  • Nucleated Red Cells (Pink): Very young red blood cells that still have their nucleus. These aren't normally found in adult blood.
  • Basophils (Yellow): A rare type of white cell that is tough enough to survive the stripping process, so it shows up as a separate, larger dot.

The Red Blood Cell Factory (RET)

What We're Looking At

This plot checks on the production of new red blood cells from your "bone marrow factory". It separates cells based on their age. Brand new red cells, called reticulocytes, still contain some leftover material from when they were made, which a special dye can light up.

  • Mature RBCs (Blue): The main population of fully grown red blood cells.
  • Reticulocytes (Pink/Red): The "baby" red blood cells. The further to the right they are, the younger they are.
  • Platelets (Purple): The small cells that help you stop bleeding also show up here.

Checking the Clotting Crew (PLT-F)

What We're Looking At

This is a special high-precision test for counting platelets, the tiny cells that form clots to stop bleeding. It's especially useful when the platelet count is very low. It works by lighting up material inside the platelets, which also allows us to see how many new, "immature" platelets are being made by the bone marrow.

  • Mature Platelets (Blue): The main population of working platelets.
  • Immature Platelets (Green): Brand new, larger platelets fresh from the bone marrow. Seeing lots of these means the body is trying hard to make more.

The Specialist Investigation (WPC)

What We're Looking At

This is a clever "double-check" that the machine runs automatically only if it spots something suspicious in the main white cell test (the WDF). It uses a different set of chemicals that affect normal and abnormal cells differently. This helps to confirm if there are genuinely worrying cells, like leukaemic blasts, or if the initial flag was just a false alarm. This saves a lot of time and helps scientists focus on the samples that really need a closer look under the microscope.
