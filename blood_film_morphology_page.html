The Art of Morphology

Beyond the numbers lies the blood film. It's here that the skilled eyes of a Biomedical Scientist can uncover the subtle clues that diagnose disease, from simple anaemias to life-threatening leukaemias.

Why and How We Examine a Blood Film

Why We Do It: The Triggers

A blood film isn't performed on every sample. It's a specialist investigation triggered when the FBC analyser flags a potential issue, or a clinician has a specific query. Common reasons include:

  • Critical FBC Results: New, unexplained low counts (neutropenia, thrombocytopenia) or very high counts.
  • Analyser Flags: Suspect flags like "Blasts?", "Atypical Lympho?", or "Fragments?".
  • Clinical Information: Specific requests such as "?Malaria", "?Haemolysis", or "?Glandular Fever".
  • Monitoring: Known haematology patients undergoing chemotherapy or treatment for leukaemia.
  • Unexplained Anaemia: To look for clues to the cause, such as specific red cell shapes or features of bone marrow failure.

How We Do It: The Process

Creating a high-quality blood film is a fundamental lab skill:

  1. Making the Smear: A small drop of EDTA blood is placed on a glass slide. A second 'spreader' slide is used at a 30-45° angle to push the blood forward, creating a thin, even smear with a "feathered edge".
  2. Staining: At NUH, we use automated Sysmex SP-50 slide maker-stainers. These use a classic Romanowsky stain (May-Grünwald-Giemsa). This involves two key components:
    • Eosin: An acidic, red/pink dye that stains basic cell components like haemoglobin and eosinophil granules.
    • Azure B (a Methylene Blue derivative): A basic, blue/purple dye that stains acidic components like DNA in nuclei and RNA in cytoplasm.
  3. Microscopy: The stained slide is now ready for systematic review under the microscope.

A Systematic Approach to Film Examination

A film review is not a random scan. We follow a strict pattern to ensure nothing is missed, starting at low power and moving to high power oil immersion to examine each cell line in detail.

Red Cell Morphology

We assess red cells for size, shape, colour, and any inclusions. We look in the "ideal" area of the film where the cells are just touching but not overlapping.

White Cell Morphology

We perform a 100-cell differential count, categorising each white cell and carefully examining for any abnormalities, such as immaturity or toxic changes.

Platelet Examination

We estimate the platelet count to confirm the analyser result and check for clumps (especially at the edges of the film). We also assess their size and granularity.

Platelet Clumps

This is the most common platelet abnormality and is usually an in-vitro artefact due to EDTA. It causes a falsely low platelet count (pseudothrombocytopenia). A fresh sample in citrate is needed.

Giant Platelets

These are platelets larger than a normal red blood cell. They are a sign of rapid platelet turnover, seen when the bone marrow is responding to a low platelet count (e.g., in ITP).

Hypogranular Platelets

Platelets can appear pale with few granules. This can be a feature of myelodysplastic syndromes (MDS) or other bone marrow disorders.

Platelet Satellitism

A rare EDTA-induced phenomenon where platelets surround neutrophils. It has no clinical significance but can cause pseudothrombocytopenia.
