Blood Film Morphology

The art of looking at blood.

When Numbers Aren't Enough

Our automated analysers provide a wealth of numerical data, but they can't see everything. When an FBC result is flagged as abnormal, or if a doctor has a specific clinical suspicion, we turn to the gold standard: the manual blood film examination.

Morphology is the study of the form and structure of cells. By looking at a stained blood film under a microscope, our highly trained Biomedical Scientists can spot abnormalities that a machine might miss, providing crucial clues for diagnosis.

What We Look For

In Red Blood Cells...

  • Size & Shape: Are they too big, too small, or abnormally shaped (e.g., sickle-shaped, tear-drop shaped)?
  • Colour: Are they pale (hypochromic), suggesting low haemoglobin content?
  • Inclusions: Are there any unusual bodies inside the cells, like Howell-Jolly bodies or malaria parasites?

In White Blood Cells...

  • Maturity: Are there immature 'blast' cells present, which can be a sign of acute leukaemia?
  • Features: Do the cells look abnormal, with features like toxic granulation (in infection) or hypersegmented nuclei (in vitamin deficiency)?
  • Proportions: Does the differential count look correct, or is one cell line dramatically increased?

In Platelets...

  • Size: Are they abnormally large or small?
  • Clumping: Are they clumping together on the slide? This can sometimes cause a falsely low platelet count from the analyser.
