Blood Transfusion: The Gift of Life

Ensuring the right blood gets to the right patient at the right time – safely and quickly.

What is the Blood Transfusion Lab?

Imagine being the vital bridge between a generous blood donor and a patient in desperate need. That's us – the Blood Transfusion team. We're like matchmakers for blood, ensuring every donation is perfectly compatible with the person who receives it. This prevents dangerous reactions and saves lives every day.

We handle everything from routine pre-surgery checks to emergency responses where seconds count. Our lab runs 24/7, testing blood samples, selecting the right blood products, and making sure they're ready when needed. We deal with red cells for oxygen transport, platelets to stop bleeding, plasma for clotting, and more specialised products.

Safety is our top priority. We follow strict rules to trace every unit from donor to patient, and we're always prepared for recalls or emergencies. Whether it's a planned operation or a sudden trauma, we're the team making sure the blood is there – and it's safe.

The Group & Screen: The Foundation of Safe Transfusion

Think of this as the essential first check before any blood transfusion. It's like verifying someone's ID before letting them into a secure area – but for blood. We need to know your blood type and check for any hidden 'troublemakers' that could cause problems.

The 'Group': Finding Your Blood Type

We test your blood to determine your ABO group (A, B, AB, or O) and whether you're RhD positive or negative. This is done in two ways:

  • Forward Typing: We mix your red blood cells with special antibodies to see which ones they react with.
  • Reverse Typing: We check your plasma for natural antibodies against A or B blood types.

Both tests must match perfectly – that's called Landsteiner's Law. If there's any mismatch or a weak reaction (like a 'weak D'), we investigate further with manual tests to get it right.

The 'Screen': Hunting for Antibodies

Your body might have developed antibodies from previous transfusions, pregnancies, or even naturally. These could attack donor blood. We mix your plasma with a panel of test red cells that have known surface markers (antigens).

If there's no reaction, it's negative – good news! If positive, we move to detailed identification. This screen is crucial because undetected antibodies can cause severe reactions during transfusion.

Samples are valid for a certain time (usually 72 hours if you've been transfused recently), so we might need fresh ones for ongoing care.

Fun fact: There are over 300 known blood group antigens, but ABO and Rh are the most important for everyday transfusions!

Antibody Testing: When the Screen Flags a Problem

If the antibody screen shows something unusual, we don't panic – we investigate! This is like solving a puzzle to identify exactly what's causing the reaction, so we can find safe blood for you.

Identification Panels

We use larger sets of test cells (like 11-cell panels) with known antigen patterns. By seeing which cells react, we can pinpoint the antibody – maybe anti-Kell, anti-Duffy, or others. Sometimes we use enzymes to enhance reactions or special techniques for tricky cases.

Titration: Measuring Strength

We dilute the plasma step by step and test each dilution. The highest dilution that still reacts gives the 'titre'. This is important for pregnant women (to assess risk to the baby) or when monitoring antibody levels over time.

Special Cases

For autoimmune issues (where antibodies attack your own cells), we do tests like the Direct Antiglobulin Test (DAT). In complex cases, we might send samples to reference labs for expert help.

Full Crossmatch vs Electronic Issue: Selecting the Perfect Match

Once we know your group and any antibodies, it's time to pick donor blood. We have two ways to do this – one hands-on, one high-tech – both ensuring safety.

Full Serological Crossmatch

The traditional method: We physically mix your plasma with a sample from the donor unit and check for reactions using gel cards or tubes. If it clumps, it's incompatible. This is used when you have antibodies or no valid historical records.

It takes longer (about 45 minutes) but is thorough. We might need to test multiple units to find the right ones.

Electronic Issue (EI)

The faster way: If your screen is negative and we have two matching historical groups, our computer system selects compatible units automatically. No physical mixing needed!

This can be done in minutes, perfect for routine cases. But strict rules apply – no antibodies, valid sample, and electronic tracking for safety.

Massive Haemorrhage Protocol: Emergency Lifesavers

In major bleeding emergencies – like accidents or childbirth complications – there's no time for full testing. We activate the Massive Haemorrhage Protocol (MHP) with a special alert system.

Immediate Response

We rush out 'universal donor' O negative red cells from emergency fridges, plus platelets and fresh frozen plasma (FFP) in pre-made packs to replace lost blood volume and clotting factors.

Ongoing Support

As we get samples, we switch to your specific blood type. We might include cryoprecipitate for extra clotting power. The goal: Stabilise the patient fast while minimising waste.

Stand-Down and Review

When bleeding is under control, we stop the protocol, return unused products, and review what happened to improve next time.

Further Testing: Digging Deeper for Special Needs

Sometimes standard tests aren't enough. We have advanced tools for unique situations, always keeping things simple and safe.

Red Cell Phenotyping

We test for extra antigens like Kell, Duffy, or Kidd. This helps find super-compatible blood for patients with multiple antibodies or those needing long-term transfusions.

Autoimmune Investigations

If your body attacks its own red cells (autoimmune haemolytic anaemia), we use tests like DAT to detect antibodies on cell surfaces. This guides treatment and blood selection.

Weak D and Variants

Some people have a 'weak' RhD that doesn't show clearly. We investigate with special tests to decide if you're treated as positive or negative – crucial for pregnancy and transfusions.

Haemolytic Disease of the Fetus and Newborn (HDFN)

For RhD negative mums, we prevent issues with anti-D injections. We test fetal RhD from mum's blood and use Kleihauer tests to check for any blood mixing. Learn more on our Antenatal Screening page.

Bone Marrow Transplant Patients

After a transplant, your blood type might change! We test donors and recipients carefully, managing any group changes post-transplant with special selection rules.

Neonatal Transfusions

Babies need extra care. We select blood that's CMV negative, irradiated if needed, and often from dedicated paediatric packs. If mum has antibodies, we choose units safe for the baby.

Blood Components: Not Just Whole Blood

One donation can help multiple patients! We separate blood into components so each patient gets exactly what they need.

Red Blood Cells

The oxygen carriers. Used for anaemia, surgery, or blood loss. Stored cold for up to 35 days.

Platelets

Stop bleeding by forming plugs. For cancer patients or those with low counts. Kept at room temperature, last 7 days.

Fresh Frozen Plasma (FFP)

Liquid with clotting factors. For major bleeds or liver issues. Frozen, thaws in special machines.

Cryoprecipitate

Concentrated clotting factors like fibrinogen. For specific bleeding disorders or emergencies.
