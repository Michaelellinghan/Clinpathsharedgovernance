The Ultimate Guide to Sample Labelling

Ensuring patient safety and timely results through accurate sample identification.

Why Labelling is Critical

Every day, our laboratory processes over 19,000 blood samples. The barcode label applied at collection is the sole identifier used by our automated analysers. Incorrect labelling is the most serious pre-analytical error and can lead to catastrophic patient outcomes. To ensure patient safety, we have a zero-tolerance policy for mislabelled or unlabelled samples.

The Gold Standard: Do's

  • Label Straight: Apply the label vertically and straight, directly over the manufacturer's label. This ensures our automated systems can read the barcode.
  • One Label Per Tube: Use only one label per tube. If additional tests are needed, request them via ICE and the lab will process the add-on.
  • Clear Barcode: Ensure the barcode is clear, well-defined, and not faded. Poor quality printing can cause read failures.
  • Allow Space: Make sure there is a gap of empty space on either side of the barcode.

Common Errors: Don'ts

  • Don't Wrap: Do not wrap the label around the tube like a flag. This makes it impossible for the barcode reader to scan.
  • Don't Obscure: Do not let the label overlap the lid or hang off the bottom of the tube. This can cause mechanical errors on the analysers.
  • Don't use multiple labels: Never apply more than one label to a tube.
  • Don't use poor quality labels: Faded or hazy barcodes will be rejected by the analysers.

Visual Guide to Labelling

DON'T: Apply crooked labels.

Analysers cannot read labels that are not straight.

DON'T: Let labels overhang.

Labels overhanging the bottom or top can cause machinery to jam or damage the label.

DO: Apply labels straight and centered.

The label should be placed directly over the manufacturer's label.

Consequences of Poor Labelling

When samples are poorly labelled, they must be manually retrieved and relabelled in the laboratory. This intervention has several serious consequences:

  • Risk of Patient Misidentification: Relabelling introduces a manual step that carries the risk of assigning the wrong label to a sample, which could have severe consequences for patient care.
  • Delayed Turnaround Times: The process of identifying, retrieving, and relabelling a sample significantly delays testing, which in turn delays the delivery of results to clinicians.
  • Delayed Diagnosis and Treatment: Delays in receiving results can lead to delays in diagnosis and the initiation of appropriate treatment for the patient.
  • Analyser Errors: Poorly applied labels can cause errors on the analysers, potentially leading to the loss of the sample entirely.
