The Ultimate Guide to Sample Labelling

Ensuring patient safety and timely results through accurate sample identification.

Why Labelling is Critical

[cite_start]Every day, our laboratory processes over 19,000 blood samples[cite: 5242]. [cite_start]The barcode label applied at collection is the sole identifier used by our automated analysers[cite: 5242, 5251]. Incorrect labelling is the most serious pre-analytical error and can lead to catastrophic patient outcomes. To ensure patient safety, we have a zero-tolerance policy for mislabelled or unlabelled samples.

The Gold Standard: Do's

    [cite_start]
  • Label Straight: Apply the label vertically and straight, directly over the manufacturer's label[cite: 5255, 5263, 5322]. This ensures our automated systems can read the barcode.
    • [cite_start]
  • One Label Per Tube: Use only one label per tube[cite: 5255, 5265]. If additional tests are needed, request them via ICE and the lab will process the add-on.
    • [cite_start]
  • Clear Barcode: Ensure the barcode is clear, well-defined, and not faded[cite: 5260, 5266, 5322]. Poor quality printing can cause read failures.
    • [cite_start]
  • Allow Space: Make sure there is a gap of empty space on either side of the barcode[cite: 5261, 5267].

Common Errors: Don'ts

    [cite_start]
  • Don't Wrap: Do not wrap the label around the tube like a flag[cite: 5255, 5324]. This makes it impossible for the barcode reader to scan.
    • [cite_start]
  • Don't Obscure: Do not let the label overlap the lid or hang off the bottom of the tube[cite: 5255, 5324]. This can cause mechanical errors on the analysers.
    • [cite_start]
  • Don't use multiple labels: Never apply more than one label to a tube[cite: 5255].
    • [cite_start]
  • Don't use poor quality labels: Faded or hazy barcodes will be rejected by the analysers[cite: 5255].

Visual Guide to Labelling

DON'T: Apply crooked labels.

[cite_start]

Analysers cannot read labels that are not straight[cite: 5245].

DON'T: Let labels overhang.

[cite_start]

Labels overhanging the bottom or top can cause machinery to jam or damage the label[cite: 5245].

DO: Apply labels straight and centered.

[cite_start]

The label should be placed directly over the manufacturer's label[cite: 5245, 5322].

Consequences of Poor Labelling

When samples are poorly labelled, they must be manually relabelled in the laboratory. [cite_start]This intervention has several serious consequences[cite: 5243, 5252]:

    [cite_start]
  • Risk of Patient Misidentification: Relabelling introduces a manual step that carries the risk of assigning the wrong label to a sample, which could have severe consequences for patient care[cite: 5247, 5253].
    • [cite_start]
  • Delayed Turnaround Times: The process of identifying, retrieving, and relabelling a sample significantly delays testing, which in turn delays the delivery of results to clinicians[cite: 5246, 5253].
    • [cite_start]
  • Delayed Diagnosis and Treatment: Delays in receiving results can lead to delays in diagnosis and the initiation of appropriate treatment for the patient[cite: 5246, 5253].
    • [cite_start]
  • Analyser Errors: Poorly applied labels can cause errors on the analysers, potentially leading to the loss of the sample entirely[cite: 5244, 5254].
