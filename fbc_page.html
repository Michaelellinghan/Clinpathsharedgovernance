The Full Blood Count (FBC)

The cornerstone of haematology. A simple test that provides a wealth of information about a patient's health, guiding diagnosis for countless conditions.

Inside the Analyser: The Sysmex XN-9100

At NUH, our Haematology labs are equipped with state-of-the-art Sysmex XN-9100 automated analysers. These are not just simple counters; they are sophisticated diagnostic systems. Let's explore the journey of a sample and the technology involved.

1. Automation

Samples are loaded in racks, barcoded, and automatically mixed and aspirated.

2. Analysis

The sample is split and sent to different channels for specific cell analysis.

3. Data Generation

Lasers and detectors generate scattergrams and histograms from tens of thousands of cells.

4. Reflex Testing

Based on results, the system can automatically make and stain a blood film for review.

Red Cells & Platelets: DC Impedance

To count the millions of red cells and platelets, the analyser uses the Direct Current (DC) impedance method. A process called hydrodynamic focusing forces cells to pass one-by-one through a tiny aperture with an electrical current. Each cell momentarily changes the resistance, creating a pulse. The number of pulses equals the cell count, and the pulse size corresponds to the cell volume (MCV). This method provides the core Red Cell and Platelet parameters.

Haemoglobin: SLS Method

The analyser uses a modern, safe, cyanide-free method to measure haemoglobin (Hb). A reagent, Sodium Lauryl Sulphate (SLS), breaks open the red cells, releasing the Hb. SLS binds to the Hb, forming a stable coloured complex. A light beam (555nm wavelength) is passed through, and the amount of light absorbed is measured, which is directly proportional to the Hb concentration.

White Cells & More: Fluorescence Flow Cytometry

This is the most advanced technology, used for the white cell differential and other specialised parameters. The sample is sent to different channels:

  • WNR Channel (White Cell Number & NRBC): Uses a harsh lysing agent to strip the cytoplasm from all cells except Nucleated Red Blood Cells (NRBCs). A fluorescent dye stains the nuclei. Flow cytometry then separates the signals, giving a highly accurate total white cell count and a count of any NRBCs present.
  • WDF Channel (White Cell Differential): Uses a gentler reagent that punches holes in the cell membranes, allowing a fluorescent dye to enter and stain the nucleic acids (DNA/RNA). When passed through the laser, the analyser measures Forward Scatter (cell size), Side Scatter (internal complexity/granularity), and Side Fluorescence (DNA/RNA content). This complex data allows it to precisely differentiate the five main types of white cells and identify immature forms like Immature Granulocytes (IGs).
  • RET Channel (Reticulocytes & Platelets): Another fluorescent dye is used here that specifically targets the residual RNA in young red cells (reticulocytes). This gives an accurate reticulocyte count, a measure of bone marrow activity. This channel also provides a fluorescent optical platelet count (PLT-O), which is vital for confirming very low platelet counts or when the impedance count may be inaccurate due to interferences like red cell fragments.

These are immature red cells that still have a nucleus. They should not be in adult peripheral blood and their presence indicates severe bone marrow stress (e.g. massive bleed, severe lack of oxygen) or marrow infiltration.

Red Blood Cell Count

High (Erythrocytosis): Can be due to dehydration or a myeloproliferative neoplasm (Polycythaemia Vera).
Low: A feature of anaemia.

HGB105g/LL

Haemoglobin

Low (Anaemia): Caused by blood loss, nutritional deficiency (iron, B12, folate), or chronic disease.

MCV72.0fLL

Mean Cell Volume

Low (Microcytic): Classic sign of iron deficiency or thalassaemia trait.
High (Macrocytic): Suggests B12/folate deficiency, liver disease, or high alcohol intake.

RDW15.5%H

Red Cell Distribution Width

Measures variation in red cell size (anisocytosis). A high RDW is a classic feature of iron deficiency, as new small cells mix with older normal-sized cells.

PLT125x10^9/LL

Platelet Count

High (Thrombocytosis): Can be a reaction to inflammation/infection or a sign of a myeloproliferative neoplasm.
Low (Thrombocytopenia): Increases bleeding risk. Caused by viral infections, drugs, autoimmune destruction (ITP), or leukaemia.

MPV12.8fLH

Mean Platelet Volume

The average size of platelets. A high MPV means platelets are larger, which often indicates the bone marrow is producing them rapidly in response to a low platelet count.

Putting It All Together: Clinical Scenarios

A single result is just a number. The true power of the FBC comes from interpreting the patterns between all the parameters. Here are some classic examples.

Patient with a Chest Infection

A patient presents with a fever and cough. Their FBC is a classic example of the body's response to a bacterial infection.

  • High WBC & High Neutrophils (Neutrophilia): The bone marrow has ramped up production of neutrophils to fight the bacteria.
  • High IG Count: The demand is so high that immature neutrophils are being released from the marrow (a 'left shift').
  • Reactive Changes: Platelets may be high as part of the acute inflammatory response. The patient may be slightly anaemic due to the 'anaemia of chronic disease'.

Patient with Iron Deficiency Anaemia

A patient reports feeling tired and breathless. Their FBC shows the typical pattern of iron deficiency.

  • Low Haemoglobin (HGB): The defining feature of anaemia.
  • Low MCV (Microcytic): Without enough iron, the bone marrow cannot produce normal-sized red cells; they become small.
  • High RDW: The mix of newly-formed small cells and older, normal-sized cells creates a wide variation in size.
  • High Platelet Count: It is common to see a mildly raised platelet count as a reactive process in iron deficiency.

Patient with Glandular Fever (EBV)

A young adult has a sore throat, fever and swollen glands. The FBC is highly suggestive of a viral infection, particularly Epstein-Barr Virus (EBV).

  • High WBC & High Lymphocytes (Lymphocytosis): The immune system is producing a large number of lymphocytes to fight the virus.
  • Atypical Lymphocytes: The key finding! These are large, reactive lymphocytes that are characteristic of glandular fever. The analyser will flag these, prompting a blood film review.
  • Mildly Low Platelets: It is common for the platelet count to dip slightly during this viral infection.
