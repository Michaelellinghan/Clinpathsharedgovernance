The Full Blood Count (FBC)

The cornerstone of haematology. A simple test that provides a wealth of information about a patient's health, guiding diagnosis for countless conditions.

Inside the Analyser: The Sysmex XN-9100

At NUH, our Haematology labs are equipped with state-of-the-art Sysmex XN-9100 automated analysers. These are not just simple counters; they are sophisticated diagnostic systems. Let's explore the journey of a sample and the technology involved.

Core Technology: Fluorescence Flow Cytometry

This is the most advanced technology, used for the white cell differential and other specialised parameters. The sample is sent to different channels:

  • WNR Channel (White Cell Number & NRBC): Uses a harsh lysing agent to strip the cytoplasm from all cells except Nucleated Red Blood Cells (NRBCs). A fluorescent dye stains the nuclei. Flow cytometry then separates the signals, giving a highly accurate total white cell count and a count of any NRBCs present.
  • WDF Channel (White Cell Differential): Uses a gentler reagent that punches holes in the cell membranes, allowing a fluorescent dye to enter and stain the nucleic acids (DNA/RNA). When passed through the laser, the analyser measures Forward Scatter (cell size), Side Scatter (internal complexity/granularity), and Side Fluorescence (DNA/RNA content). This complex data allows it to precisely differentiate the five main types of white cells and identify immature forms like Immature Granulocytes (IGs).
  • RET Channel (Reticulocytes & Platelets): Another fluorescent dye is used here that specifically targets the residual RNA in young red cells (reticulocytes). This gives an accurate reticulocyte count, a measure of bone marrow activity. This channel also provides a fluorescent optical platelet count (PLT-O), which is vital for confirming very low platelet counts or when the impedance count may be inaccurate due to interferences like red cell fragments.

Red Cells & Platelets: DC Impedance

To count the millions of red cells and platelets, the analyser uses the Direct Current (DC) impedance method. Hydrodynamic focusing forces the cells to pass one-by-one through a tiny aperture with an electrical current. Each cell momentarily changes the resistance, creating a pulse. The number of pulses equals the cell count, and the pulse size corresponds to the cell volume (MCV).

Haemoglobin: SLS Method

The analyser uses a modern, safe, cyanide-free method. A reagent, Sodium Lauryl Sulphate (SLS), breaks open the red cells, releasing the Hb. SLS binds to the Hb, forming a stable coloured complex. A light beam (555nm wavelength) is passed through, and the amount of light absorbed is measured, which is directly proportional to the Hb concentration.

Decoding the Data: Scattergrams & Histograms

The analyser translates the signals from tens of thousands of individual cells into graphical plots. These plots allow Biomedical Scientists to visually inspect the cell populations and spot abnormalities that numerical results alone might miss.

WDF Scattergram

Plots white cells based on internal complexity (Side Scatter) and DNA/RNA content (Side Fluorescence). This is key for identifying abnormal populations.

RBC & PLT Histograms

These graphs show the size distribution of red cells and platelets. Abnormal shapes or multiple populations are easily identified.

Understanding Analyser Flags

The analyser uses a complex algorithm to flag results that are abnormal or potentially inaccurate. These flags are our cue to perform further investigation, such as a blood film review. The table below summarises some of the most common flags and what they mean for us in the lab.

Flag MessagePotential Cause(s)Laboratory Action
Blasts/Abn Lympho?Presence of blast cells (acute leukaemia) or abnormal lymphocytes (lymphoma, viral infections).Urgent blood film review is mandatory. Refer to Haematology Registrar.
Atypical Lympho?Reactive lymphocytes, commonly seen in glandular fever (EBV) or other viral illnesses.Perform blood film review. Suggest Monospot test if appropriate.

PLT Clumps?Platelets have clumped together in the EDTA tube (an in-vitro artefact).Check sample for a clot. Perform blood film review to confirm clumping and estimate platelet count. A repeat sample in a citrate (blue top) tube is required for an accurate count.

Putting It All Together: Clinical Scenarios

The true power of the FBC comes from interpreting the patterns between all the parameters. Here are some classic examples.

Patient with a Chest Infection

A classic example of the body's response to a bacterial infection.

  • High WBC & High Neutrophils (Neutrophilia): The bone marrow has ramped up production of neutrophils to fight the bacteria.
  • High IG Count: The demand is so high that immature neutrophils are being released (a 'left shift').
  • Reactive Changes: Platelets may be high as part of the inflammatory response.

Patient with Iron Deficiency Anaemia

The typical pattern of iron deficiency.

  • Low Haemoglobin (HGB) & Low MCV (Microcytic): Without enough iron, red cells become small and pale.
  • High RDW: The mix of new small cells and older normal cells creates a wide variation in size.
  • High Platelet Count: It is common to see a reactive thrombocytosis in iron deficiency.

Patient with Glandular Fever (EBV)

Highly suggestive of a viral infection, particularly Epstein-Barr Virus (EBV).

  • High WBC & High Lymphocytes (Lymphocytosis): The immune system is producing a large number of lymphocytes to fight the virus.
  • Atypical Lymphocytes: The key finding! These large, reactive lymphocytes are characteristic of glandular fever.

Patient with Acute Myeloid Leukaemia (AML)

A medical emergency requiring immediate action.

  • Pancytopenia: The rapid proliferation of malignant myeloblasts in the bone marrow crowds out all normal cell production.
  • Blasts on film: The defining feature. Often with Auer Rods.

Patient with Chronic Lymphocytic Leukaemia (CLL)

Often an incidental finding in older adults.

  • Marked Lymphocytosis: A persistent, very high count of mature-looking lymphocytes is the key finding.
  • Smear Cells: The malignant cells are fragile and often rupture during film preparation, creating characteristic "smear" or "smudge" cells.

Patient with Chronic Myeloid Leukaemia (CML)

A myeloproliferative neoplasm with a characteristic blood picture.

  • Very High WBC: A dramatic increase in white cells, often >100 x10⁹/L.
  • Marked Left Shift: The film shows the full spectrum of granulocyte maturation, from myeloblasts to mature neutrophils.
  • Basophilia: An increased basophil count is a classic and vital feature for diagnosis.
